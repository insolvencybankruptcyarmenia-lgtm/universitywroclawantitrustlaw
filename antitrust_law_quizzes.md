## Short Answer Practice

1. Explain the difference between Article 101 and Article 102 in one paragraph.

2. Why is defining the relevant market essential before assessing dominance?

3. Give three examples of hardcore restrictions under Article 101.

4. Why does a leniency programme make cartels unstable?

5. Explain why dominance is not illegal by itself.

6. What is the difference between excessive pricing and predatory pricing?

7. How can tying allow a dominant company to extend power from one market into another?

8. What is a margin squeeze, and why does it matter in infrastructure markets?

9. When can state compulsion protect an undertaking from liability?

10. In an exam problem, why should you ask "who is worse off and why?"

## Model Answers: Short Answer Practice

1. Article 101 targets coordination between two or more undertakings, such as agreements, concerted practices, cartels, price fixing, or market sharing. Article 102 targets unilateral conduct by a dominant undertaking, such as predatory pricing, tying, loyalty rebates, refusal to deal, or margin squeeze. Article 101 may allow exemption under Article 101(3), while Article 102 has no equivalent exemption once abuse is proven.

2. Market definition determines the competitive space in which power and effects are assessed. Without a product and geographic market, it is impossible to know whether the undertaking is dominant, whether customers have substitutes, or whether conduct significantly restricts competition.

3. Examples include price fixing, output limitation, market sharing, customer allocation, and bid rigging.

4. Leniency creates mistrust inside the cartel. Because the first member to confess may receive immunity or reduced fines, each member fears that another will report first. This makes continued cartel cooperation risky.

5. Dominance only means the undertaking has substantial market power and can act independently of competitors or customers. Competition law prohibits the abuse of that power, not the mere possession of it.

6. Excessive pricing means charging unfairly high prices, usually exploiting customers. Predatory pricing means charging very low prices to drive competitors out, then later recovering losses through higher prices.

7. Tying uses power in one market to influence another. If customers need dominant product A and are forced or strongly pressured to buy product B as well, competitors in product B may be excluded even though the dominant company was not originally dominant in B.

8. A margin squeeze happens when a vertically integrated dominant firm controls an essential wholesale input and also competes downstream. If it sets wholesale access prices high and retail prices low, downstream rivals cannot earn a viable margin.

9. State compulsion may protect an undertaking where the state legally required the specific anticompetitive conduct and the undertaking had no real legal room to act differently.

10. The question identifies the economic mechanism of harm. It helps distinguish normal competition from abuse or collusion by focusing on who is harmed, how they are harmed, and whether the conduct reduces competition rather than merely hurting a competitor.

## Exam Drill: Identify the Issue

For each fact pattern, identify the likely provision and issue.

1. Competitors exchange future price plans at an annual trade fair.
2. A dominant supplier gives customers rebates only if they buy almost all requirements from it.
3. A public agency has an exclusive legal right to provide employment placement services but cannot meet demand.
4. A dominant platform refuses access to a rival, but the rival can build or use an alternative platform.
5. A producer bans all online sales through its selective distribution system.
6. A dominant company lowers prices below cost only on routes where a new entrant appears.
7. A group of publishers jointly controls an important distributor and cuts off a competing distributor.
8. A dominant machine producer requires customers to buy spare parts only from it.
9. A local farmer can sell apples only to one nearby supermarket chain.
10. Two companies coordinate bids in a public tender.

### Suggested Answers: Exam Drill

1. Article 101; possible concerted practice/information exchange.
2. Article 102; loyalty rebates and foreclosure.
3. Article 102 with Article 106; state monopoly may still involve an undertaking if the activity is economic.
4. Article 102 refusal to deal likely weak because indispensability is missing.
5. Potential Article 101 vertical restraint; Article 102 only if dominance exists.
6. Article 102 predatory pricing if dominance is established.
7. Article 102 collective dominance/exclusionary abuse; Article 101 may also be considered if concerted action is proven.
8. Article 102 tying/aftermarket restriction if dominance and separate demand are established.
9. Possible superior bargaining power issue in jurisdictions that recognise it; not pure EU dominance unless market definition supports dominance.
10. Article 101 bid rigging/cartel conduct.
