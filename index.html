§1 — The Concept of Undertaking & Scope of Competition Law

Definition of Undertaking

Core Definition

An undertaking is any entity, regardless of its legal form, that carries out economic activity, defined as offering goods or services on the market, regardless of whether for profit.

What IS economic activity

  • Offering goods/services on the market
  • Public services (transport, utilities) — even state-run
  • Military unit renting billboard space on its premises
  • Hospital renting conference rooms
  • Church/school charging for services
  • A single one-off activity qualifies

What is NOT economic activity

  • Functions restricted exclusively to state authority (police, military combat, judiciary, tax collection)
  • Activities that cannot legally be performed without state mandate
  • Social solidarity systems (e.g., compulsory national insurance with redistribution, no choice for members)
Key Principles
  • Profit not required — non-profit entities can be undertakings
  • Legal form irrelevant — NGO, state entity, church, private company: same test
  • Each activity assessed individually — one entity can be an undertaking for some activities but not others
  • State can outsource state functions — outsourcing does not make a non-economic activity economic
  • The test is always: "is the activity offering goods/services on the market?"

Distinguishing State vs. Economic Activity

ActivityEconomic?Reason
Police patrolNoRestricted to state; involves public compulsion
State tram companyYesAnyone could run trams (with approval); state does it by choice
Air traffic control (outsourced)NoEven outsourced, involves exclusive state powers
Compulsory employment service (Höfner case)YesPrivate actors can and do provide recruitment services
Military unit renting land for mobile antennaYes (for that activity)Offering a service on the market

§2 — Forms of Collusive Behaviour (Article 101)

Three Categories of Coordination

1. Agreements

Written or oral contracts. Paper trail exists. Easiest to detect and prove. Assessment is purely substantive.

2. Decisions of Associations

Trade/lobby groups used as coordination platform. Members coordinate via the association without direct contact. E.g., truck manufacturers sharing discount data via trade association.

3. Concerted Practices

Everything else. No paper trail, no formalized agreement. Hardest to prove. Companies align behaviour without explicit agreement.

The Challenge of Concerted Practices

Companies acting in concert will avoid leaving any paper trail. The problem is that parallel behaviour (e.g., all raise prices on the same day) may look like collusion but can be explained as rational independent reactions to market signals.

The Parallel Conduct Problem

Company A raises prices Monday. Company B raises prices Tuesday. Company C raises prices Wednesday.

Could be: collusion OR rational parallel conduct (each reacting to the others).

As long as an alternative explanation exists, the burden of proof on the claimant is not met.

Tacit Collusion

Collusion where no company explicitly commits to any course of action — all simply assume the others will behave consistently. Very difficult to prove. Common in oligopolistic markets.

Truck Manufacturers Case (Information Exchange)

Major truck producers shared data via trade association: production volumes, sales volumes, and actual discounts (not just list prices). Discounts in this market reach 80%.

Pro-collusion argument: By knowing competitors' discounts, everyone will match them → customer stuck at same price level.

Counter-argument: By knowing competitors' discounts, you can undercut them and win business → pro-competitive.

Key question: What does the rest of the market picture say? (See §3 on stability factors.)

§3 — Detecting Concerted Practices: Prisoner's Dilemma & Stability

The Prisoner's Dilemma Applied to Cartels

ScenarioCompany ACompany BOutcome
Both stay silent1 year1 yearBest collective result
A confesses, B silentGoes free20 yearsBest for A individually
Both confess10 years10 yearsBest for authorities
Leniency — Breaking the Cartel

The competition authority incentivises cooperation by offering reduction or elimination of fines to the first company to confess. Because cartel members don't know if others are talking, there is strong incentive to cooperate first. This is the primary enforcement tool against cartels.

  • First to confess: 100% fine reduction possible
  • Later cooperators: partial reduction
  • Creates a race to the competition authority

Three Conditions for Cartel Stability

These factors help assess whether collusion is likely and sustainable. None is conclusive alone. They must be assessed together.

1

Benefit from opportunistic behaviour < Long-term benefit from staying

Conducive to defection: Markets with few, large-value orders (military procurement, nuclear equipment). Each single order is worth defecting for.

Conducive to stability: Markets with many small transactions (convenience goods, chips). Sustaining the arrangement over time is more valuable than any single deal.

Example: Micro-chips at 1 cent each bought in millions → many small contracts → stability. Military jet contract for 20 years → defect.

2

Transparency of relations

If cartel members can detect early that a member is considering defection (via information-sharing, price monitoring, etc.), they can act before defection is complete. High transparency → stable cartel.

3

Credible retaliation mechanism

Members must be able to punish a defector. Indicators:

  • Relative size of companies (similar size → collective retaliation possible)
  • Supply/distribution interdependence (member A supplies input to member B → can cut supply)
  • Shared logistics, warehousing, distribution networks
Exam Tip: IC Integrated Circuits Case

Annual trade fair; companies share discount data and sign contracts. Very cheap chips (1 cent each), bought by a few large buyers in bulk. The market structure (few large buyers) means each bulk contract is very valuable → creates incentive to defect (like military market). This undermines cartel stability even within a high-volume market.

§4 — Hardcore Restrictions & Balancing (Art. 101(1)–(3))

Article 101(1) — The Prohibited Categories

Article 101(1) TFEU — Hardcore Restrictions

Agreements/decisions/concerted practices that cannot be balanced or justified:

  1. Directly or indirectly fix purchase or selling prices or any other trading conditions
  2. Limit or control production, markets, technical development, or investment
  3. Share markets or sources of supply
  4. Apply dissimilar conditions to equivalent transactions, placing trading parties at a competitive disadvantage
  5. Make contracts subject to supplementary obligations having no connection with the subject of the contracts
✓ Article 101(3) — Exemption Conditions (ALL must be met)
  1. Contributes to improving production or distribution, or promoting technical/economic progress
  2. Allows consumers a fair share of the resulting benefit
  3. Restrictions must be indispensable to the objectives
  4. Must not afford the possibility of eliminating competition on a substantial part of the market
✗ Cannot use Art. 101(3) if:
  • Hardcore restriction present → full stop, no balancing
  • Positive effects on one market cannot justify a hardcore restriction in another (even if overall positive)
  • One company is left outside two groups → eliminates competition

Key Cases Under Art. 101

Elevator & Escalator Manufacturers (Otis, Schindler, Kone, Thyssen)
CONDUCT

(1) Shared technical data + standardised parts → better service/parts market. (2) Market-sharing on construction projects ("I take this project, you take that one").

ANALYSIS

Market sharing (part 2): Clear hardcore restriction. End of story for that part.

Technical data sharing (part 1): Pro-competitive argument — better choice of service providers. BUT: it creates market foreclosure in the product market (buyers of non-colluding brands get inferior after-sales options). The two practices are inseparable → if one part has an unjustifiable effect, the whole arrangement fails.

LESSON

If a practice has inseparable effects across markets, and one market effect cannot be justified, the entire practice fails. Positive effects on the service market cannot save a hardcore restriction in the product market.

Airlines (Copenhagen/Stockholm) — Network Coordination
CONDUCT

Two airlines combine networks: common tickets, loyalty programme, baggage transfer, schedule coordination. Drop some direct routes; gain new indirect connections.

MARKET DEFINITION IS DECISIVE

If each route = separate market → dropping CPH-STK direct while only one carrier remains = market sharing (hardcore). If Helsinki-London overall = the market → no change in competition on that market.

LESSON

In aviation cases, relevant market is typically city-pair (point-to-point) from the demand side. Even if there are large transfer passenger flows, if even some passengers fly point-to-point, that market exists. Existence of a hardcore restriction on even one city-pair route sinks the whole arrangement.

Treuhandanstalt (Troy Fund) — State Entity as Cartel Facilitator
CONDUCT

Post-reunification German state asset manager sat buyers of industrial machinery down and told them: "There are 10 machines, 5 of you — agree among yourselves who buys how many." Organised market-sharing without being a market participant itself.

LESSON

A non-market participant can facilitate a hardcore restriction. The companies sitting at the table committed market-sharing. The enabling body (trade association, government entity, third-party platform) can also be liable even though it has no business interest in the outcome.

Van Eycke — State-Endorsed Private Price-Fixing
CONDUCT

Belgian legislation allowed private professional bodies (ASPA) to formulate pricing rules for spirits, which then received legal force through state approval. Rules influenced commercial conditions across the market.

KEY POINT

No state compulsion: the rules were not adopted purely by the state — private sector participants participated in their development. Therefore, the undertakings cannot rely on state compulsion as a defence. They brought it upon themselves. The price-fixing was committed by the undertakings, not mandated by the state.

§5 — Dominant Position: Definition & Categories of Abuse (Art. 102)

Definition of Dominant Position

Legal Definition

A dominant position is the ability to act on the market unilaterally — without needing to take into account the reactions of competitors or customers.

What dominance is

  • NOT defined by a specific market share percentage
  • ~75% market share: practically always dominant
  • ~20% can be dominant in fragmented markets
  • Always relative to the number and strength of other players
  • Can be individual (single dominant company) OR collective (group acting together)

What dominance is NOT

  • Not the same as monopoly (monopoly always = dominant; dominant ≠ monopoly)
  • Not prohibited per se — having a dominant position is legal
  • Not automatically abusive
  • Often reflects superior business performance or inherited market position (post-communist utilities)

Categories of Abuse

Exclusionary Practices

Aimed at competitors. Goal: exclude them from the market or keep them marginalised.

  • Predatory pricing
  • Margin squeeze
  • Tying and bundling
  • Loyalty rebates (if exclusionary)
  • Refusal to deal
  • Market foreclosure via selective discount
Exploitative Practices

Aimed at customers/clients. Goal: extract maximum value because clients have nowhere to go.

  • Excessive pricing
  • Unfair trading conditions
  • Discriminatory pricing between equivalent transactions
⚠️ Critical Rule: No Exceptions for Art. 102 Abuses

Unlike Art. 101(3), there is no balancing act and no exemption for abuses of dominant position. If abuse is proven, it is automatically prohibited. Period. This is why the distinction between Art. 101 and Art. 102 matters enormously: the same conduct may be justifiable under 101(3) but automatically prohibited under 102.

Collective Dominant Position

A group of undertakings, none of which individually holds a dominant position, can collectively hold dominance when taken together they achieve a critical mass of market power and act in concert. Assessment follows the same collusion analysis from Art. 101 — you must prove they are acting together. If collective dominance is established, Art. 102 applies to their joint conduct.

§6 — Exploitative Abuses: Excessive & Predatory Pricing

Excessive Pricing

Definition

A price is excessive when it has no reasonable relationship to the economic value of the product/service — i.e., the markup is disproportionate to the cost of production.

Greek Airports — General Aviation Charges
FACTS

New operator (Fraport) took over Greek airports and overnight raised charges for general aviation (small planes, pilot schools, parachute clubs) by 600–800%. Competitor airlines wanted rid of them as they blocked runways.

RELEVANT MARKET

Single airport = the relevant market. Pilot schools cannot move to another airport 200 km away. Business is tied to that location. Without geographic flexibility, the airport has monopoly power over those users.

EXCLUSIONARY OR EXPLOITATIVE?

Exclusionary — purpose was to drive out general aviation to improve conditions for mainline airlines. 600% is not what you charge to extract more money from customers you want to keep; it is what you charge to eliminate them.

THE ENFORCEMENT PROBLEM

Excessive pricing is theoretically recognisable but practically unenforceable. To say "this price is excessive," you must say what price is not excessive — which immediately introduces price control. This paradox makes excessive pricing one of the rarest successful claims in competition law.

The Sorites Paradox of Excessive Pricing

If 600% is excessive, is 550%? Is 300%? Is 150%? Wherever you draw the line is arbitrary. And once you draw it, everyone will price up to that line. Price regulation results. This is why authorities almost never pursue excessive pricing despite recognising it exists.

Helsingborg Ferry Terminal — Port Charges (Art. 102 Excessive Pricing)

Port operator charged ferry operators significantly more than comparable ports. Ferry operators dependent on this terminal (only crossing point without a multi-hour detour). Case: prices were high but authority could not establish what a non-excessive price would be without introducing price control. Complaint dismissed. Illustrates the enforcement paradox.

Predatory Pricing

Definition

A dominant company lowers prices dramatically around the time a new competitor enters, with the objective of driving the entrant out. The dominant company can sustain losses longer than the entrant. Once the entrant exits, prices return to supra-competitive levels.

Predation vs. Dumping: Dumping = prices below cost (predatory by definition). Predatory pricing = prices close enough to the entrant's to split demand and inflict losses, but may still be above cost. The key is the pattern and timing, not a single price snapshot.

Key Detection Requirement

Predation can only be detected by observing a price trend over time correlated with the entrant's market entry. A snapshot of prices at any one moment shows nothing. Continuous market monitoring is essential.

Capacity Dumping

A variant: the dominant company increases production/capacity beyond demand when a new entrant arrives. This floods the market, reduces everyone's sales, and imposes losses on all. Dominant company outlasts the entrant because it has more capital reserves.

Example: Ryanair adding a flight immediately before and after EasyJet's Dublin-Liverpool service to "sandwich" the competitor.

Ryanair / EasyJet — Dublin Routes

EasyJet enters Dublin-UK routes. Ryanair: (1) lowered prices on overlapping routes, (2) added extra frequency to "sandwich" EasyJet's schedule. Creates demand split → everyone loses money → Ryanair with bigger reserves outlasts EasyJet. In absence of dominant position: perfectly legal healthy competition. With dominant position: exclusionary abuse.

§7 — Exclusionary Abuses: Tying, Bundling & Loyalty Rebates

Tying and Bundling

Mechanism

Company has dominant position in Product A. Does not have dominance in Product B. Leverages dominance in A to gain advantage in B by forcing customers who buy A to also buy B.

Key Test: Separate Demand

Products are considered separate (and therefore cannot be bundled by a dominant company) if there is separate, independent demand for each product. The existence of separate demand is the deciding criterion.

If no separate demand → naturally one product → bundling acceptable.

Hilti — Nail Guns, Nails & Cartridges
DOMINANT POSITION IN

Nail guns (invented the market; near-monopoly initially)

NO DOMINANT POSITION IN

Nails and cartridges (many third-party producers)

CONDUCT

(1) Told distributors: stop selling third-party nails, or we stop supplying you with our guns and give you discounts for compliance. (2) Told customers: using third-party nails voids your warranty.

DEFENDANT'S ARGUMENT

"Third-party nails are inferior and unsafe. We are protecting consumers."

RULING / LESSON

Even if true, it is not the company's role to act as market regulator. If third-party products are legally sold and meet safety standards, the dominant company cannot unilaterally restrict their use. Both practices = abusive.

Microsoft — Windows & Internet Explorer

Microsoft pre-installed Internet Explorer with Windows. Browsers at the time were paid products (Netscape). By bundling a free browser, Microsoft shut out competitors. Separate demand existed: consumers actively purchased browsers separately.

Consequence: Now when setting up Windows, users see a browser choice screen. This is the direct result of the competition case.

Irony: Microsoft dominated by tying, but then lost the browser market anyway because IE was such a poor product — dominance acquired by abuse is fragile.

Tetra Pak — Carton Machinery & Packaging

Tetra Pak: monopoly in aseptic carton filling machines. No dominance in packaging materials (many suppliers). Strategy: "We'll give you free maintenance/training/upgrades — if you use only our packaging." Framed as an incentive (carrot), not a threat (stick). Analysis: same coercive effect. Carrot or stick, leveraging machine dominance to capture the packaging market = abuse.

Loyalty Rebates / Selective Discounts

When Discounts Are Legal
  • Non-dominant companies: virtually always fine
  • Volume-based (sell more, get more): generally fine even for dominant companies
  • Transparent, objective, non-discriminatory criteria: better argument
When Discounts Are Abusive (Art. 102)
  • Criteria set specifically so only one company qualifies (Ryanair 185-seat threshold)
  • Discounts contingent on exclusivity or limits on use of competitors
  • Used to prevent customers from switching to new entrants
  • Discount is predatory in effect (drives entrant prices below competition)
Ryanair — Airport Loyalty Rebates (Regional Airport Case)

Regional airport offered discounts for airlines with planes of 185+ seats. Ryanair: 187 seats. EasyJet Airbus A220: 184 seats. The 185-seat threshold was set deliberately so only Ryanair qualifies. Result: Ryanair gets a discount, EasyJet doesn't → higher effective cost for EasyJet → market foreclosure.

Airport's defence: "Criteria are objective and transparent — anyone with 185+ seat plane gets the discount." Newer EU case law is more nuanced: if all facts show only one company could realistically ever meet the criteria, the formal neutrality is not sufficient.

British Airways — Travel Agency Commissions

BA paid travel agents progressively higher commissions as ticket sales exceeded thresholds. Agents incentivised to maximise BA ticket sales → minimise competitor sales by implication. Without dominant position: perfectly legal business incentive. With dominant position (BA had dominant position in the UK air ticket market): the mechanism forecloses the market to competitors.

Algida Ice Cream — Fridge Tying

Algida provided free refrigerators to small shops with condition: 80%+ of display space for Algida products. In supermarkets: no problem, space for multiple fridges. In small convenience stores (target market): only room for one fridge. Either take the free Algida fridge (and be bound) or buy your own (incurring cost). Outcome: captive shelf space. Exclusionary abuse.

Swedish Snus (SNUS) — Labelling Requirements

Dominant SNUS company gave free refrigerators to bars/restaurants. Condition: all products displayed in the fridge must follow specific labelling guidelines (where to place price, logo, taste descriptor). Technically allowed competitors' products in the fridge. Case took 20 years and was never satisfactorily resolved — because there was no clear mechanism of harm. Neither dominant company's profits nor competitors' losses could be causally linked to the labelling rule. This case illustrates: abuse of dominant position normally involves a clear economic mechanism. If you cannot identify the mechanism, there may be no abuse.

§8 — Infrastructure & Network Abuses: Margin Squeeze

The Margin Squeeze

Context

Occurs in network industries (telecoms, railways, electricity, water) where access to physical infrastructure is essential for competing. Former state monopolies often own this infrastructure and simultaneously provide services over it.

Dominant firm owns infrastructure
→
Competitor must use infrastructure to operate
→
Dominant firm raises infrastructure access price
→
Competitor's costs rise; margin is squeezed
→
Competitor cannot invest or expand → exits

Natural Monopoly

Infrastructure that cannot be replicated (physical impossibility or prohibitive cost) has natural monopoly status. No one can build a second railway network or second electricity grid. Therefore, access conditions are critical for competition downstream.

Unbundling — The Regulatory Solution

Unbundling (Structural Separation)

Organisationally separating the infrastructure owner from the service provider using that infrastructure. When the infrastructure owner no longer has a stake in downstream competition, their interest becomes maximising access revenue → they want to let everyone in up to capacity.

  • Must be combined with transparent, non-discriminatory access pricing (controlled by regulator)
  • Same access price for everyone, including the former dominant operator's successor
  • Removes the economic motivation for margin squeeze

Applied to: European telecoms, railway operation, electricity markets.

Slot Allocation — A Related Problem

Airport landing slots = time-limited access to infrastructure. Dominant incumbent airlines can hold more slots than needed, blocking competitors from entry without "doing" anything obviously exclusionary. Hard to distinguish between: (a) legitimate use of legitimately obtained slots, and (b) blocking access artificially.

Refusal to Deal & Indispensability

Indispensability Test

A dominant company's refusal to grant access to its infrastructure/platform/network is abusive only if that access is indispensable for carrying out the activity — meaning there is no viable alternative.

This is a very high bar: "helpful" or "significantly beneficial" does not equal "indispensable." There must be no realistic substitute or self-provision option.

Superior Bargaining Power

Concept in some jurisdictions (Germany, Poland for food sector): a company need not have overall market dominance to abuse its position. If in a specific bilateral trading relationship, one party is entirely dependent on the other (e.g., apple farmer selling only to one local supermarket chain), there may be a power imbalance actionable as "superior bargaining power." Not universal — controversial and absent from pure EU competition law. In Poland: applies specifically to agri-food sector.

Augsburg Airways / Lufthansa — 100% Client Dependency

Augsburg Airways operated 8 aircraft entirely on behalf of Lufthansa under codeshare. 100% of revenue from one client. Lufthansa cancelled. Augsburg had no brand, no direct sales capability, no other routes — filed for bankruptcy within 24 hours.

Lesson: Superior bargaining power claims are weakened when the dependent party made a business decision to concentrate all operations with one client. The thin line between "abuse of power" and "consequences of imprudent business strategy" is central to the doctrine.

§9 — State Action, State Compulsion & Non-Economic Activity

State Compulsion Doctrine

Definition

An undertaking is not liable for a violation of competition rules if the specific anticompetitive behaviour was mandated by the state and the undertaking had no legal room for manoeuvre — i.e., the only legally permissible course of action was the anticompetitive one.

In that case, the state bears liability, not the undertaking.

State Compulsion Applies When
  • State unilaterally adopts a law mandating the conduct
  • Undertaking has zero room to comply with competition law
  • The only legal course of action is the anticompetitive one
State Compulsion Does NOT Apply When
  • Undertakings participated in drafting the rules (Van Eycke)
  • Undertakings could have proposed alternative approaches
  • State "incentivised" but did not mandate the behaviour
  • Undertakings had any room for manoeuvre whatsoever

State Decides Where Markets Exist

Key Principle: No Right to a Competitive Market

If the state decides from the outset that there will be only one operator in a given sector (e.g., a public employment agency), there is no "abuse" — because the state has the right to determine whether a market exists at all. There is no automatic right to market entry or competition.

Abuse would arise only if: (a) the state permits multiple operators in principle but then uses discriminatory barriers against specific entrants, or (b) the single operator goes beyond the state mandate to harm competitors through its own conduct.

Höfner Case (C-41/90) — Compulsory Employment Agencies

German law gave the Bundesanstalt für Arbeit a legal monopoly on employment placement. Private recruitment firms were banned. Court held the Bundesanstalt was nonetheless an undertaking because placement services can be, and in other contexts are, provided by private entities. The activity is economic (offered on the market). The monopoly might constitute an abuse of dominant position if the agency could not meet demand — creating a situation where the exclusive right was in breach of Art. 102 taken together with Art. 106 TFEU.

§10 — Relevant Market: Product & Geographic

Why Relevant Market Is Always First

Market Definition is Threshold Issue

Without establishing the relevant market, it is impossible to assess: (1) whether a dominant position exists, or (2) whether an agreement has an effect on trade between member states. Get the market wrong → the entire case collapses.

Product Market — Demand-Side Substitutability

SSNIP Test (Small but Significant Non-transitory Increase in Price)

A product B is in the same market as product A if, when the price of A increases by 5–10%, a sufficient number of consumers switch to B that the price increase becomes unprofitable. The key question is always: from the consumer's perspective, are these products substitutable?

SituationMarket Definition Implication
Beer only vs. all alcoholIf consumers don't switch to vodka when beer gets more expensive → beer is a separate market → dominance easier to establish
Single route in aviation (CPH-LHR)If consumer wants to fly CPH-LHR, adding more Helsinki connections is irrelevant → each route = separate market
Long product life (elevators, 20 years)Always creates connected aftermarket: spare parts + maintenance. Must define multiple markets.
Small shops in local areaGeographic market may be local: farmer selling to the only local supermarket → that supermarket = dominant buyer for that transaction
Airport for general aviationSingle airport = relevant market if businesses cannot feasibly relocate (pilot school tied to its location)

Geographic Market

The geographic market comprises the area where the conditions of competition are homogeneous and sufficiently different from neighbouring areas. Key factors:

  • Ability of suppliers to relocate
  • Ability of buyers to source from elsewhere
  • Transport costs relative to product value
  • Regulatory differences between territories
Bayer — Pharma Parallel Trade Spain/UK

Bayer's medicine priced low in Spain, high in UK. Spanish pharmacists buying extra and re-selling to UK. Bayer tried to stop this by limiting supply to Spanish pharmacies or conditioning supply on no-resale terms.

Geographic market: Spain + UK = one market, because the goods were freely substitutable and were actively being traded across the border.

Art. 101 or 102? Looked like a bilateral agreement (Bayer + Spanish pharmacies). But if Spanish pharmacies had no real choice (dominant supplier, no alternatives), the "agreement" was really a unilateral act of the dominant company. Therefore → Art. 102 analysis.

Nature: Exploitative toward UK customers (forced to pay higher prices) AND restricting parallel trade (harming EU internal market integration).

Beer Case — Belgium, Netherlands, France (Diageo type)

Beer priced lower in Netherlands than Belgium. Dutch distributors buy cheap and re-sell to Belgium. Dominant brewery limits supply to Dutch distributors to prevent this arbitrage.

Market definition debate: Beer only (→ dominance likely) vs. all alcohol (→ no dominance). Argument for wider market: substitutability between beer, wine, cider. Counter: different consumption patterns, price points, occasions.

Art. 101 or 102? If Dutch distributors had no choice but to accept Diageo's conditions (dominant), it's Art. 102 unilateral abuse. If they participated willingly → Art. 101 collusion.

Abuse: Limiting supply to prevent lawful parallel trade = limiting market to prejudice of consumers (Art. 102(b)) + possible discriminatory conditions (Art. 102(c)).

§11 — Case-Solving Framework (Exam Technique)

Universal Step-by-Step Method

1

Identify the parties and their activities

Who is doing what? Who is upstream (producers), who is downstream (distributors/retailers)? Are they competitors (horizontal) or in a supply chain (vertical)?

2

Define the relevant market (product + geographic)

This is almost always the most decisive step. Use demand-side substitutability. Multiple markets possible (e.g., product + aftermarket). Getting this wrong invalidates the rest of the analysis.

3

Choose the applicable provision: Art. 101 or Art. 102?

Art. 101 if:
  • More than one undertaking involved
  • All parties benefiting and could refuse
  • Concerted action on horizontal or vertical basis
Art. 102 if:
  • Single dominant undertaking OR collective dominance
  • Partner had no real ability to refuse (forced into "agreement")
  • Unilateral conduct imposing conditions

Same conduct can fall under both. Art. 102 is the final safety net — no exceptions. Always check 102 even if 101 clears the conduct.

4

If Art. 101: Check for hardcore restrictions first

Price fixing, market sharing, production limits, dissimilar conditions, supplementary obligations = automatic prohibition. If none present → proceed to 101(3) balancing.

5

If Art. 102: Identify type of abuse

Exclusionary (aimed at competitors) or exploitative (aimed at customers)? Follow the money: who benefits from the conduct and at whose expense?

If abuse established → automatically prohibited, no exceptions.

6

Consider defences and alternative arguments

  • Non-economic activity (activity restricted to state authority)?
  • State compulsion (no room for manoeuvre)?
  • Objective justification (safety, quality — but must be proportionate)?
  • Art. 101(3) exemption (if no hardcore restriction)?

"Follow the Money" Shortcut

Quick Assessment Tool

For any competition case, ask: Who is worse off and why?

  • Competitors being harmed → exclusionary abuse (Art. 102) or market foreclosure (Art. 101)
  • Customers paying more/getting less → exploitative abuse (Art. 102)
  • Everyone in the group benefiting → likely collusion (Art. 101), not unilateral
  • Dominant company unilaterally gains while partner has no benefit → Art. 102 unilateral conduct

IC Chips (Annual Trade Fair)Annual data exchange + contract signingFew large buyers → each contract is high value → incentive to defect. Undermines cartel stability. No clear evidence of stable collusion.
TreuhandanstaltState asset manager coordinated market sharing by buyersNon-market participant can facilitate hardcore restriction. No business motive needed to be liable. Undertakings at the table = liable.
Van Eycke (Belgian Spirits)State-approved but privately drafted pricing rulesNo state compulsion: private actors participated in drafting. Undertakings are liable for the resulting price-fixing. State compulsion is black-and-white: 0 room for manoeuvre required.
Bayer — Spain/UK PharmaSupply restrictions to prevent parallel tradeLooked like bilateral agreement but pharmacies had no real choice (dominant supplier) → unilateral action of dominant company → Art. 102. Exploitative to UK customers; restricts internal market integration.
Hilti (Nail Guns)Tying gun + nails; voiding warranty for third-party nailsSeparate demand = separate products. Safety defence rejected: not the company's role to act as regulator. Both practices = exclusionary abuse.
Microsoft — IEBrowser pre-installed with OSSeparate demand for browsers existed. Pre-installation = illegal bundling. Consequence: mandatory browser choice screen on Windows.
Tetra PakFree maintenance/upgrades if use only Tetra Pak packagingCarrot (incentive) = same anticompetitive effect as stick (threat). Leveraging machine monopoly into packaging market = abuse.
Algida Ice CreamFree fridge with condition: 80% Algida productsNo physical space for second fridge in small shops = exclusive shelf space. Foreclosure of competitor ice cream brands = abuse.
British Airways — CommissionsProgressive performance bonuses for travel agents selling BA ticketsWithout dominant position: legal incentive scheme. With dominant position: forecloses market to competitor airlines via travel agents.
Greek Airports — General Aviation600–800% price increase overnight for small aircraftAirport = relevant market (businesses cannot relocate). Exclusionary abuse (drive out general aviation to help mainline airlines). Excessive pricing = practically unenforced due to price control paradox.
Ryanair — Dublin/UK routesPrice cutting + capacity dumping on EasyJet-overlapping routesTextbook predatory pricing + capacity dumping. Detection requires observing price trend at time of new entry, not price snapshot.
Helsingborg Ferry TerminalPort charges significantly above comparable portsExcessive pricing recognisable in principle but no enforcement possible without specifying acceptable price level = price control. Complaint dismissed.
Swedish SNUSLabelling requirements on products in free fridges20-year case resolved inconclusive because no identifiable mechanism of harm. Key lesson: abuse requires an identifiable economic mechanism of harm.
Steam / Valve (Geo-blocking)Activation keys blocked by geography; different national pricesMarket sharing = hardcore restriction (EU, this market). Game publishers co-defendants because they benefited from price segmentation → Art. 101 (not Art. 102, since both sides benefit). Geo-blocking = market sharing = automatic prohibition.
Pierre Fabre (Cosmetics)Selective distribution: pharmacy-only, no online salesWith non-dominant company + no discriminatory/anti-competitive criteria → vertical restraint permissible. Would be collusive only if pharmacies and producer agreed to exclude online rivals. Art. 102 requires dominant position first.
British Sugar / Napier BrownUpstream producer + downstream distributor; vertical structureThree markets: production, distribution, retail. Conduct by dominant upstream player using intrapress subsidiary to exclude competitor distributor = collective dominant position + exclusionary abuse.
Apple App StoreVetting apps + mandatory Apple payment processor for in-app purchasesOngoing/unresolved. Key issues: (1) vetting discretion = potential arbitrary exclusion; (2) payment processing tying. Apple's defence: transparent, non-discriminatory rules applicable to all. Outcome pending.
Lepin Bricks (Lego knockoff)Lepin Club, full-line forcing, rebates, product placementDominant position assumed. If all distributors benefit → Art. 101 collusion → Art. 101(3) balancing possible (pro-consumer: better access, full range). If distributors had no choice → Art. 102 → no exemption → foreclosure of competing brick brands.
Belgian Book Distribution (Brusila)4 publishers owning Brusila distributor; cut off competitor distributor IntrapressCollective dominant position (51% combined). Publishers held meeting to coordinate response = evidence of concerted action. Refusal to supply = exclusionary abuse (Art. 102). Art. 101 market-sharing argument weaker (didn't technically divide market). State compulsion: none.
Diageo Beer (Belgium/Netherlands)Supply limits to prevent parallel trade from NL to BEGeographic market = BE + NL (substitutable goods, active trade). Dominant position → Art. 102. Limiting supply = limits market to prejudice of consumers (Art. 102(b)) + discriminatory conditions (Art. 102(c)).
Höfner (C-41/90)State employment agency with legal monopolyEven state entities carrying out economic activity = undertakings. Legal monopoly + inability to meet demand = potential abuse under Art. 102 + Art. 106 TFEU.

Fast Reference: Defences That Work vs. Don't Work

Defences That Can Work
  • Non-economic activity (true state function restricted by law)
  • State compulsion (0% room for manoeuvre)
  • Art. 101(3) (no hardcore restriction + 4 criteria met)
  • Objective justification for Art. 102 (safety/quality, but proportionate + not company's role to regulate)
  • No dominant position (if market defined broadly enough)
  • Conduct was unilateral of dominant company (turns 101 issue into 102 issue → may or may not be abuse)
Defences That Do NOT Work
  • "We are a public company / state-owned" (irrelevant if economic activity)
  • "We didn't earn a profit" (profit not required for economic activity)
  • "The state incentivised us" (partial — must be full compulsion)
  • "Third-party products are inferior" to justify tying (not the company's role to regulate)
  • "Our criteria are formally neutral" if only one company realistically qualifies
  • Art. 101(3) to justify a hardcore restriction
  • Any argument to justify Art. 102 abuse (no exceptions)

§13 — Quiz Practice

Multiple Choice Review

Core Concepts

  1. An undertaking is best defined as:
    1. Any registered company
    2. Any entity engaged in economic activity
    3. Any public body
    4. Any entity with legal personality
    AnswerB. Economic activity is the key test, regardless of legal form or profit.
  2. Economic activity means:
    1. Activity performed only by private companies
    2. Activity offered on a market for goods or services
    3. Activity funded by taxation
    4. Activity performed without profit
    AnswerB. The activity must involve offering goods or services on a market.
  3. Which activity is most likely non-economic?
    1. Selling insurance policies
    2. Operating a commercial airport
    3. Exercising public police powers
    4. Selling software licenses
    AnswerC. Core public authority powers are normally non-economic.
  4. The relevant market includes:
    1. Only the company's preferred business area
    2. Product and geographic dimensions
    3. Only the country where the company is registered
    4. Only the cheapest substitute products
    AnswerB. Both product and geographic market must be defined.

Article 101

  1. Which is a classic hardcore restriction?
    1. Price fixing between competitors
    2. Hiring more employees
    3. Launching a new product
    4. Lowering prices independently
    AnswerA. Price fixing is a hardcore Article 101 restriction.
  2. Market sharing means:
    1. Competitors divide customers, territories, or routes
    2. A company expands into a new country
    3. Consumers compare suppliers
    4. A dominant firm refuses infrastructure access
    AnswerA. Dividing markets between competitors restricts competition by object.
  3. Leniency destabilises cartels because it:
    1. Forces firms to raise prices
    2. Rewards the first cartel member to confess
    3. Bans all vertical agreements
    4. Requires consumers to sue first
    AnswerB. It creates a race to the competition authority.
  4. A stable cartel is helped by:
    1. Easy detection of cheating
    2. Many anonymous one-off buyers
    3. No retaliation mechanism
    4. Large rewards from secret undercutting
    AnswerA. Transparency makes cheating easier to detect and punish.

Article 102

  1. Article 102 applies where:
    1. There is any agreement between companies
    2. A dominant undertaking abuses its position
    3. The company is state-owned
    4. The market is global
    AnswerB. Dominance plus abuse is required.
  2. Predatory pricing involves:
    1. A dominant undertaking lowering prices to drive out competitors
    2. Two competitors agreeing to raise prices
    3. The state setting maximum prices
    4. Consumers switching suppliers
    AnswerA. The exclusionary strategy is to sacrifice short-term profit to remove rivals.
  3. Tying occurs where:
    1. A dominant firm in product A forces customers to also buy product B
    2. Two firms divide territories
    3. A company sells one product at a discount
    4. A state creates a regulator
    AnswerA. Dominance in one product is leveraged into another product market.
  4. A margin squeeze occurs when:
    1. A vertically integrated dominant firm sets wholesale and retail prices so rivals cannot compete profitably
    2. Two retailers agree to charge the same price
    3. Consumers refuse high prices
    4. A supplier improves product quality
    AnswerA. The rival is squeezed between input price and downstream retail price.

Case Scenarios

  1. Four bus companies agree each will operate only in a different city district. Best issue?
    1. Article 101 market sharing
    2. Article 102 excessive pricing
    3. State compulsion
    4. Non-economic activity
    AnswerA. Competitors are dividing territories.
  2. A dominant operating system provider makes it difficult to choose rival browsers. Best issue?
    1. Tying or bundling abuse
    2. State aid
    3. Non-economic activity
    4. Leniency
    AnswerA. Dominance in one product may be used to restrict competition in another.
  3. A railway infrastructure owner charges rivals high track access prices while keeping its own retail fares low. Best issue?
    1. Margin squeeze
    2. Market sharing
    3. Tacit collusion
    4. State compulsion
    AnswerA. Rivals may be unable to compete downstream profitably.
  4. A supplier refuses access to an input, but realistic substitutes exist. Refusal to deal is likely:
    1. Always abusive
    2. Weak because access is not indispensable
    3. Always illegal under Article 101
    4. Irrelevant to competition law
    AnswerB. Indispensability is central to refusal-to-deal analysis.

  3. Give three examples of hardcore Article 101 restrictions.
  4. Why does leniency make cartels unstable?

Exam Drill

For any scenario, answer in this order:

  1. Identify the undertaking and economic activity.
  2. Define the product and geographic market.
  3. Choose Article 101 or Article 102.
  4. Identify the restriction or abuse.
  5. Explain who is worse off and why.
  6. Consider defences, exemptions, or state compulsion.

Exam Strategy Reminders

  1. Always start with relevant market — getting it right changes everything downstream.
  2. Art. 102 is the last safety net — check it even if Art. 101 analysis is inconclusive.
  3. No exceptions for abuses — once you identify abuse under Art. 102, it's done.
  4. Follow the money — who benefits and who is harmed identifies the mechanism.
  5. Identify the mechanism — if you can't explain how the conduct causes harm, there may be no abuse (SNUS case).
  6. Distinguish 101 from 102 by asking: could the "partner" have refused? If yes → collusion (101). If no → unilateral dominant conduct (102).
  7. Hardcore restrictions → end of 101 balancing exercise immediately.
  8. State compulsion → must be absolute (zero room to manoeuvre). Participation in rule-making = no compulsion.
  9. Separate demand test for tying — if consumers independently buy the two products, they are separate products.
  10. Long product life → always look for aftermarkets (parts + services).
